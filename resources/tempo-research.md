# Tempo in Resistance Training: Research Summary

## Decision: Do Not Track Tempo

After reviewing the literature, we decided **not** to track rep tempo in Iron Cadence. The logging friction outweighs the minimal value it provides. Our existing load/volume/RIR tracking captures the meaningful training variables.

## Key Findings

### 1. Tempo Has Minimal Impact on Hypertrophy

Within normal training ranges (0.5-8 seconds per rep), tempo produces equivalent hypertrophy outcomes. The muscle responds to mechanical tension and proximity to failure, not the speed of the rep.

- Very slow tempos (>10s per rep) can reduce hypertrophy because lifters must use significantly less load
- Extremely fast/ballistic reps may reduce time under tension enough to matter, but this is uncommon in standard hypertrophy training
- Eccentric-focused tempos (e.g., 4-second negatives) increase muscle damage but do not reliably produce greater hypertrophy than normal tempos

### 2. No Major App Tracks Tempo

Strong, Hevy, RP Hypertrophy, and other leading workout apps do not include tempo as a core logged variable. Some allow optional tempo prescriptions on templates, but none require or encourage logging actual tempo performed.

### 3. PRs Should Not Be Tempo-Specific

A 225 lb bench press is a 225 lb bench press. Fragmenting PRs by tempo (e.g., "225 lb at 3-1-2 tempo" vs "225 lb at 2-0-1 tempo") would create confusion and dilute the motivational value of personal records.

### 4. Tempo Changes Are Largely Unconscious

Lifters naturally slow down as they approach failure within a set. This is an inherent part of training to a given RIR and does not need to be tracked separately. Attempting to log actual tempo performed adds significant friction for data that is:

- Hard to measure accurately without sensors
- Highly variable rep-to-rep within the same set
- Not actionable for programming decisions

### 5. When Tempo Matters (Edge Cases)

Tempo prescriptions can be useful as **coaching cues**:

- Paused reps (e.g., paused bench press) to eliminate stretch reflex
- Slow eccentrics during injury rehabilitation
- Controlled negatives during deload weeks

These are better handled as exercise variations (e.g., "Paused Bench Press" as a separate catalog entry) rather than a tempo field on every set.

## If We Ever Revisit This

If tempo support is added in the future, it should be:

- An **optional display cue** on exercise templates (e.g., "slow eccentric"), not a logged data point
- Never required for completing a set
- Not factored into PR calculations
- Not used in volume or fatigue calculations

## Sources

1. Schoenfeld, B.J., Ogborn, D.I., & Krieger, J.W. (2015). "Effect of repetition duration during resistance training on muscle hypertrophy: a systematic review and meta-analysis." *Sports Medicine*, 45(4), 577-585. — Meta-analysis finding no significant difference in hypertrophy across rep durations of 0.5-8 seconds.

2. Wilk, M., et al. (2020). "Does tempo of resistance exercise impact training volume?" *Journal of Human Kinetics*, 73, 241-252. — Slower tempos reduce total volume lifted per session, which may offset any theoretical benefit.

3. Hackett, D.A., et al. (2018). "Effect of movement velocity during resistance training on muscle-specific hypertrophy." *European Journal of Sport Science*, 18(3), 396-402. — No significant differences in muscle thickness between fast and slow tempo groups.

4. Headley, S.A., et al. (2011). "Effects of lifting tempo on one repetition maximum and hormonal responses to a bench press protocol." *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research*, 25(2), 406-413. — Tempo manipulation did not meaningfully alter strength or hormonal responses.

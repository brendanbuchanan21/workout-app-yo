# Progressive Overload for Hypertrophy: Research Review

## Central Question

What does the evidence say about the optimal ORDER of progression variables
(load, reps, volume) for maximizing hypertrophy?

---

## 1. FOUNDATIONAL EVIDENCE: Volume-Hypertrophy Dose-Response

### Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2017) - The Landmark Meta-Analysis

- **Citation:** Schoenfeld BJ, Ogborn D, Krieger JW. Dose-response relationship
  between weekly resistance training volume and increases in muscle mass: A
  systematic review and meta-analysis. J Sports Sci. 2017;35(11):1073-1082.
- **Design:** Systematic review and meta-regression, 34 treatment groups from
  15 studies
- **Key findings:**
  - Graded dose-response: each additional weekly set = +0.023 effect size
    (+0.37% gain)
  - Higher vs lower volume difference: ES = 0.241 (3.9% greater gains)
  - Trend across categories (<5, 5-9, 10+ sets/muscle/week), p = 0.074
- **Limitation:** Most included studies used untrained subjects. Only 2 of 15
  studies used trained individuals.
- **Implication for our question:** Volume clearly matters. More sets = more
  growth, at least up to a point. But where is that point?

### Krieger (2010) - Single vs Multiple Sets

- **Citation:** Krieger JW. Single vs. multiple sets of resistance exercise for
  muscle hypertrophy: a meta-analysis. J Strength Cond Res. 2010;24(4):1150-9.
- **Design:** Multilevel meta-regression, 55 effect sizes from 8 studies
- **Key findings:**
  - Multiple sets = 40% greater hypertrophy effect sizes than single sets
  - 2-3 sets trended better than 1 set; 4-6 sets trended better than 1 set
  - No significant difference between 2-3 sets and 4-6 sets per exercise
- **Implication:** Diminishing returns begin relatively early at the per-session
  level (around 4-6 sets per exercise).

### Schoenfeld et al. (2019) - 1 vs 3 vs 5 Sets in Trained Men

- **Citation:** Schoenfeld BJ, Contreras B, Krieger J, et al. Resistance training
  volume enhances muscle hypertrophy but not strength in trained men. Med Sci
  Sports Exerc. 2019;51(1):94-103.
- **Design:** RCT, 34 resistance-trained men, 8 weeks, 3 groups
  - 1SET: 6-9 sets/muscle/week
  - 3SET: 18-27 sets/muscle/week
  - 5SET: 30-45 sets/muscle/week
- **Key findings:**
  - All groups increased strength similarly (no volume advantage for strength)
  - Higher volume conditions showed significantly greater hypertrophy at
    elbow flexors, mid-thigh, and lateral thigh
  - 5SET showed advantages over 1SET, supporting the dose-response for
    hypertrophy even at very high volumes (30-45 sets/week)
- **Limitation:** 8-week duration. Sustainability of 45 sets/week is
  questionable long-term.

### Pelland, Remmert, Robinson, Hinson & Zourdos (2026) - Latest Meta-Regression

- **Citation:** Pelland JC et al. The resistance training dose response:
  Meta-regressions exploring the effects of weekly volume and frequency on
  muscle hypertrophy and strength gains. Sports Med. 2026;56:481-505.
- **Design:** Meta-regression, 67 studies, 2,058 participants, 220 hypertrophy
  effects and 490 strength effects
- **Key findings:**
  - 100% posterior probability that volume has a positive effect on both
    hypertrophy and strength
  - Both show diminishing returns, but diminishing returns are MORE
    PRONOUNCED for strength than hypertrophy
  - Frequency had negligible/inconsistent effect on hypertrophy
  - Frequency had a positive effect on strength (100% posterior probability)
  - At average volume of 12.25 sets/week: +0.24% hypertrophy per additional set
  - Distinguished "direct" vs "indirect" sets (0.5 weighting for indirect)
- **Implication:** Volume continues to drive hypertrophy with diminishing returns
  well past moderate volumes. Frequency matters less for size than for strength.

### Baz-Valle et al. (2022) - Systematic Review

- **Citation:** Baz-Valle E, Balsalobre-Fernandez C, et al. A systematic review
  of the effects of different resistance training volumes on muscle hypertrophy.
  J Hum Kinet. 2022;81:199-210.
- **Design:** Systematic review, literature search across 3 databases
- **Key findings:**
  - 12-20 weekly sets per muscle group recommended for trained young men
  - 20+ sets showed additional hypertrophy for triceps but not biceps or quads
  - Substantial diminishing returns beyond ~12-20 sets/muscle/week
- **Implication:** The "optimal" volume zone is approximately 12-20 sets/week,
  with individual variation.

## 2. EVIDENCE ON DIMINISHING RETURNS AND "JUNK VOLUME"

### Amirthalingam et al. (2017) - German Volume Training

- **Citation:** Amirthalingam T, Mavros Y, Wilson GC, et al. Effects of a
  modified German volume training program on muscular hypertrophy and strength.
  J Strength Cond Res. 2017;31(11):3109-3119.
- **Design:** RCT, 19 healthy men, 6 weeks, 10x10 vs 5x10 split routine
  3x/week
- **Key findings:**
  - 5 sets produced EQUAL OR BETTER hypertrophy than 10 sets
  - 5-set group had superior trunk/arm lean mass and greater strength gains
  - Authors recommend 4-6 sets per exercise to maximize hypertrophy
- **Implication:** Per-session volume has a ceiling. Beyond ~5-6 sets per
  exercise per session, additional sets may become counterproductive (more
  fatigue, no additional stimulus).

### Ostrowski et al. (1997) - Volume Comparison

- **Citation:** Ostrowski KJ, Wilson GJ, Weatherby R, Murphy PW, Lyttle AD.
  The effect of weight training volume on hormonal output and muscular size
  and function. J Strength Cond Res. 1997;11(3):148-154.
- **Design:** RCT, 27 men with 1-4 years training, 10 weeks, 3 groups:
  3 sets/week, 6 sets/week, 12 sets/week per muscle group
- **Key findings:**
  - All groups significantly increased size, strength, and power
  - No significant between-group differences
  - Triceps growth: 2.3% (3 sets), 4.7% (6 sets), 4.8% (12 sets)
  - Doubling from 6 to 12 sets produced negligible additional gains
- **Implication:** Early evidence of diminishing returns. Going from 6 to 12
  sets/week doubled fatigue but not gains.

### Haun et al. (2019) - Extreme Volume Study

- **Citation:** Haun CT, Vann CG, Mobley CB, et al. Effects of graded whey
  supplementation during extreme-volume resistance training. Front Nutr.
  2018;5:84.
- **Related:** Haun CT et al. Muscle fiber hypertrophy in response to 6 weeks
  of high-volume resistance training in trained young men is largely attributed
  to sarcoplasmic hypertrophy. PLoS ONE. 2019;14(6):e0215267.
- **Design:** 6-week progressive volume protocol in trained men, ramping from
  10 to 32 sets/week per exercise, 3 days/week
- **Key findings:**
  - Subjects continued to hypertrophy even at extreme volumes (32 sets/exercise)
  - Did not clearly surpass MRV on average
  - Hypertrophy was largely sarcoplasmic rather than myofibrillar
  - High inter-individual variability in response
- **Implication:** Some individuals can tolerate and respond to extreme volumes,
  but the hypertrophy may be qualitatively different (sarcoplasmic). MRV varies
  enormously between individuals.

### Heaselgrave et al. (2019) - Inverted U Relationship

- **Citation:** Heaselgrave SR, Blacker J, Smeuninx B, McKendry J, Breen L.
  Dose-response relationship of weekly resistance-training volume and frequency
  on muscular adaptations in trained men. Int J Sports Physiol Perform.
  2019;14(3):360-368.
- **Design:** RCT, 49 trained men, 6 weeks, biceps training at 9, 18, or 27
  sets/week
- **Key findings:**
  - Muscle thickness changes: +0.1cm (9 sets), +0.3cm (18 sets), +0.2cm
    (27 sets)
  - No significant differences, but pattern suggests inverted U
  - 18 sets may be a "sweet spot" with 27 sets showing no additional benefit
- **Implication:** Supports the idea that beyond a threshold, more volume
  does not equal more growth and may even reduce it.

## 3. LOAD vs REPS: DOES IT MATTER HOW YOU PROGRESS?

### Plotkin et al. (2022) - Load Progression vs Rep Progression

- **Citation:** Plotkin DL, Roberts MD, Haun CT, Schoenfeld BJ. Progressive
  overload without progressing load? The effects of load or repetition
  progression on muscular adaptations. PeerJ. 2022;10:e14142.
- **Design:** RCT, 43 resistance-trained individuals (27M, 16F), 8 weeks
  - LOAD group (n=21): increased weight, maintained 8-12 rep range
  - REPS group (n=17): increased reps, kept load constant
  - 4 lower body exercises, 4 sets, 2x/week, training to failure
- **Key findings:**
  - Similar hypertrophy across all measured muscles
  - Rectus femoris showed modest advantage for REPS group (2.8mm difference)
  - Strength gains similar (~20kg squat improvement in both)
  - Muscular endurance improved similarly
- **Conclusion:** "Both progressions of repetitions and load appear to be
  viable strategies for enhancing muscular adaptations."
- **Implication:** For hypertrophy, the METHOD of progression (load vs reps)
  matters less than the fact that SOME progression occurs.

### Chaves et al. (2024) - Load vs Rep Progression

- **Citation:** Chaves TS, et al. Effects of resistance training overload
  progression protocols on strength and muscle mass. Int J Sports Med.
  2024;45(6):425-432.
- **Design:** RCT, 39 untrained young adults (20M, 19F), 10 weeks, leg
  extension
  - LOADprog: progressive increase in weight
  - REPSprog: progressive increase in repetitions
- **Key findings:**
  - Both increased 1RM similarly (LOADprog: 52.9->69.1kg; REPSprog: 51.7->66.8kg)
  - Both increased vastus lateralis CSA similarly (LOADprog: 21.3->23.5cm2;
    REPSprog: 21.1->23.4cm2)
  - No significant between-group differences for either outcome
- **Implication:** Confirms Plotkin 2022. The type of progression variable
  manipulated (load vs reps) does not meaningfully affect hypertrophy outcomes.

### Nobrega/Scarpelli et al. (2022) - Volume Load Progression Models

- **Citation:** Nobrega SR, Scarpelli MC, Barcelos C, Chaves TS, Libardi CA.
  Muscle hypertrophy is affected by volume load progression models. J Strength
  Cond Res. 2023;37(1):62-67.
- **Design:** RCT, 24 untrained men, ~24 sessions
  - %1RM group: traditional percentage-based loading
  - RM Zone group: autoregulated rep-range-based loading
- **Key findings:**
  - RM Zone group had greater rate of volume load progression (2.30% vs 1.01%
    per session)
  - RM Zone group had greater gains in muscle CSA
  - No differences in total volume load or strength gains
- **Implication:** HOW you structure progression matters. Autoregulated
  approaches that allow natural volume load progression may outperform rigid
  percentage-based models for hypertrophy.

## 4. LOAD RANGE AND HYPERTROPHY

### Schoenfeld et al. (2017) - High vs Low Load Meta-Analysis

- **Citation:** Schoenfeld BJ, Grgic J, Ogborn D, Krieger JW. Strength and
  hypertrophy adaptations between low- vs. high-load resistance training: A
  systematic review and meta-analysis. J Strength Cond Res. 2017;31(12):
  3508-3523.
- **Design:** Systematic review and meta-analysis, 21 studies, loads <=60% vs
  >60% 1RM, all sets to failure, minimum 6 weeks
- **Key findings:**
  - Hypertrophy: SIMILAR between high and low loads
  - Strength (1RM): significantly greater with high loads
  - Isometric strength: no difference
- **Implication:** Load is not a primary driver of hypertrophy. You can grow
  with 30-85% 1RM if effort is sufficient.

### Schoenfeld et al. (2021) - Re-Examination of the Repetition Continuum

- **Citation:** Schoenfeld BJ, Grgic J, Van Every DW, Plotkin DL. Loading
  recommendations for muscle strength, hypertrophy, and local endurance: A
  re-examination of the repetition continuum. Sports. 2021;9(2):32.
- **Design:** Narrative review
- **Key findings:**
  - The traditional "hypertrophy zone" (6-12 reps) is not uniquely
    hypertrophic
  - Trivial ES difference (0.03) between high and low loads for whole muscle
    hypertrophy
  - Similar hypertrophy across wide loading spectrum (>=30% 1RM) when
    training to or near failure
  - Heavy loads ARE superior for maximal strength
  - Light loads are superior for muscular endurance
- **Implication:** The "rep range" for hypertrophy is much wider than
  traditionally believed. This means load progression within a mesocycle is
  less critical for hypertrophy than previously thought.

## 5. PROXIMITY TO FAILURE

### Refalo et al. (2023) - Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

- **Citation:** Refalo MC, Helms ER, Trexler ET, Hamilton DL, Fyfe JJ.
  Influence of resistance training proximity-to-failure on skeletal muscle
  hypertrophy: A systematic review with meta-analysis. Sports Med.
  2023;53(3):649-665.
- **Design:** Systematic review with meta-analysis, 15 studies
- **Key findings:**
  - Trivial advantage for training to set failure vs non-failure (ES = 0.19,
    95% CI: 0.00-0.37)
  - No moderating effect of volume load or relative load
  - Training to failure is NOT clearly superior to training near failure
    (1-2 RIR) for hypertrophy
- **Implication:** You do not need to train to absolute failure, but you need
  to be CLOSE (within ~1-3 RIR). This affects fatigue management.

### Robinson et al. (2024) - Proximity to Failure Meta-Regression

- **Citation:** Robinson ZP, Pelland JC, Remmert JF, et al. Exploring the
  dose-response relationship between estimated resistance training proximity
  to failure, strength gain, and muscle hypertrophy: A series of
  meta-regressions. Sports Med. 2024;54(9):2373-2386.
- **Design:** Meta-regression
- **Key findings:**
  - Hypertrophy significantly increases as sets are performed closer to failure
  - Strength gains are similar across a wide range of RIR
  - The dose-response for proximity-to-failure differs between strength and
    hypertrophy
- **Implication:** Proximity to failure is a more important variable for
  hypertrophy than for strength. "Hard sets" (close to failure) are the
  currency of hypertrophy, not just total sets.

## 6. MECHANICAL TENSION AS PRIMARY MECHANISM

### Schoenfeld (2010) - Mechanisms of Hypertrophy

- **Citation:** Schoenfeld BJ. The mechanisms of muscle hypertrophy and their
  application to resistance training. J Strength Cond Res. 2010;24(10):
  2857-2872.
- **Design:** Narrative review
- **Key findings:**
  - Three proposed mechanisms: mechanical tension, metabolic stress, muscle
    damage
  - Mechanical tension identified as the primary driver
  - Tension produced by both force generation and stretch
  - Mechanotransduction converts mechanical signals to molecular events
- **Subsequent evolution:** By 2023-2024, the consensus has shifted further:
  - Muscle damage is now considered largely irrelevant or even detrimental
  - Metabolic stress may be an independent stimulus but likely secondary
  - Mechanical tension remains the primary accepted mechanism
- **Implication:** The "active ingredient" in training is mechanical tension
  applied to muscle fibers. This can be achieved through various load/rep
  combinations as long as motor units are fully recruited (near failure).

## 7. PERIODIZATION FOR HYPERTROPHY

### Grgic et al. (2017) - Linear vs Undulating Periodization

- **Citation:** Grgic J, Mikulic P, Podnar H, Pedisic Z. Effects of linear
  and daily undulating periodized resistance training programs on measures of
  muscle hypertrophy: a systematic review and meta-analysis. PeerJ.
  2017;5:e3695.
- **Design:** Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Key findings:**
  - No difference in muscle hypertrophy between LP and DUP
  - When volume-equated, periodization model does not affect hypertrophy
  - DUP may have small advantage for strength in trained individuals
- **Implication:** The STRUCTURE of periodization (linear vs undulating) matters
  less than total volume for hypertrophy.

### Moesgaard et al. (2022) - Volume-Equated Periodization

- **Citation:** Moesgaard L, Beck MM, Christiansen L, et al. Effects of
  periodization on strength and muscle hypertrophy in volume-equated resistance
  training programs: A systematic review and meta-analysis. Sports Med.
  2022;52(7):1647-1666.
- **Key findings:**
  - Periodization does not significantly affect hypertrophy in
    volume-equated designs
  - Total training volume is the more important variable
- **Implication:** For hypertrophy, worry about volume first, periodization
  structure second.

## 8. INDIVIDUAL VARIATION AND RECOVERY

### Scarpelli et al. (2020, 2022) - Individualized Volume

- **Citation:** Scarpelli MC, Nobrega SR, Santanielo N, et al. Muscle
  hypertrophy response is affected by previous resistance training volume in
  trained individuals. J Strength Cond Res. 2022;36(4):1153-1157.
- **Design:** Within-subject RCT, 16 trained individuals, one leg standardized
  (22 sets/wk), other leg individualized (1.2x previous volume)
- **Key findings:**
  - Individualized volume (based on previous training history) produced
    superior hypertrophy vs standardized volume
  - Even modest increase (+20%) in volume above habitual levels drove
    additional growth
- **Implication:** Volume needs to be individually calibrated. What matters is
  progression ABOVE your current adapted level, not an absolute number.

### Wernbom et al. (2007) - Quantitative Analysis

- **Citation:** Wernbom M, Augustsson J, Thomee R. The influence of frequency,
  intensity, volume and mode of strength training on whole muscle
  cross-sectional area in humans. Sports Med. 2007;37(3):225-264.
- **Design:** Comprehensive quantitative review
- **Key findings:**
  - Given sufficient frequency, intensity, and volume, all training modes can
    induce significant hypertrophy
  - High inter-individual variability in optimal dose
  - No single mode or type of muscle action is clearly superior
- **Implication:** There is no one-size-fits-all prescription. Individual
  response variation is substantial.

## 9. THE KEY PAPER: Israetel et al. (2019) - Mesocycle Progression

### Israetel, Feather, Faleiro & Gentil (2019)

- **Citation:** Israetel MA, Feather J, Faleiro TV, Gentil P. Mesocycle
  progression in hypertrophy: Volume versus intensity. Strength Cond J.
  2021;43(4):45-51.
- **Design:** Narrative review / expert opinion piece
- **Key argument:**
  - Three ways to progress within a mesocycle: add weight, add reps, add sets
  - **Their recommendation: prioritize set (volume) progression** as the
    primary driver within a mesocycle
  - Reasoning: the dose-response between volume and hypertrophy is well
    established, while load range has minimal impact on hypertrophy
  - Intensity progression should be secondary
  - "Some of all 3" is ideal, but set progression is "probably being the most
    well supported"
- **Their model:**
  - Start mesocycle at MEV (Minimum Effective Volume)
  - Ramp volume weekly through MAV (Maximum Adaptive Volume)
  - Approach MRV (Maximum Recoverable Volume) in final week
  - Keep load relatively stable or modestly increasing
  - Deload to MV (Maintenance Volume), then repeat
- **Limitation:** This is a narrative review, not an RCT. No direct
  experimental comparison of volume-first vs load-first progression exists.
- **Implication:** The RP model of volume-first progression is well-reasoned
  from existing evidence, but is ultimately an extrapolation.

## 10. SYNTHESIS: What Does the Evidence Actually Say?

### What We Know With High Confidence

1. **Volume has a dose-response relationship with hypertrophy** (Schoenfeld
   2017, Krieger 2010, Pelland 2026). More hard sets = more growth, with
   diminishing returns.

2. **Load range does not significantly affect hypertrophy** (Schoenfeld 2017,
   2021). Whether you use 30% or 85% 1RM, hypertrophy is similar if effort
   is equated.

3. **Proximity to failure matters for hypertrophy** (Refalo 2023, Robinson
   2024). Sets need to be "hard" (within ~1-3 RIR) to count toward the
   hypertrophic stimulus.

4. **Per-session volume has diminishing returns around 4-6 sets per exercise**
   (Amirthalingam 2017, Krieger 2010). Spreading volume across more sessions
   may be more productive than cramming it into fewer.

5. **Load progression and rep progression produce equivalent hypertrophy**
   (Plotkin 2022, Chaves 2024). The type of overload matters less than its
   presence.

6. **Individual variation in optimal volume is substantial** (Haun 2019,
   Scarpelli 2022, Heaselgrave 2019). MRV can vary enormously.

7. **Periodization structure matters less than total volume** for hypertrophy
   (Grgic 2017, Moesgaard 2022).

### What We Do NOT Know (Gaps in Evidence)

1. **No study has directly compared volume-first vs load-first vs hybrid
   progression models** for hypertrophy over matched durations. This is the
   most critical gap.

2. **Long-term studies (>12 weeks) on progression strategies are almost
   nonexistent.** Most evidence comes from 6-10 week studies.

3. **Trained populations are underrepresented.** Much volume-hypertrophy
   research uses untrained or lightly trained subjects.

4. **The interaction between progression variables is unstudied.** We do not
   know if sequencing (e.g., reps first, then load, then volume) produces
   different outcomes than other orderings.

### Answering the Central Question

**"What is the optimal order of progression variables for hypertrophy?"**

The honest answer is: **we do not have direct evidence for any specific
ordering.** However, we can make evidence-informed inferences:

#### The Case for Volume-First (RP Model)

- Supported by: Schoenfeld 2017 dose-response, Pelland 2026 meta-regression,
  Israetel 2019 narrative review
- Logic: Since volume has the strongest dose-response with hypertrophy, and
  load range barely matters, it makes sense to prioritize the variable with
  the strongest evidence base
- Practical advantage: Easy to implement (add 1 set/week), clear progression
  metric, allows for periodic deloads via volume reduction
- Risk: May reach diminishing returns and excessive fatigue if volume climbs
  too high without load progression

#### The Case for Load/Rep-First (Double Progression)

- Supported by: Plotkin 2022 and Chaves 2024 showing equivalent hypertrophy
  from load or rep progression
- Logic: Increasing load or reps increases mechanical tension per set, which
  may allow you to achieve the same stimulus with fewer total sets (better
  stimulus-to-fatigue ratio)
- Practical advantage: Familiar, intuitive, self-limiting (you stop adding
  reps/load when you cannot), works well for strength AND hypertrophy
  simultaneously
- Risk: Trained lifters plateau quickly on load progression, limiting its
  long-term utility as the sole progression method

#### The Case for Hybrid Approaches

- Supported by: Israetel 2019 ("some of all 3"), Nobrega/Scarpelli 2022
  (autoregulated progression outperformed rigid progression)
- Logic: No single variable should be pushed to its limit while others
  stagnate. Rotating emphasis prevents plateaus.
- A practical hybrid model:
  1. **Within a session:** Push reps toward top of range (effort progression)
  2. **Between sessions:** When top of rep range is reached, add load (double
     progression)
  3. **Across weeks in a mesocycle:** Add 1-2 sets per muscle group per week
     (volume progression)
  4. **Between mesocycles:** Reset volume to MEV, potentially with higher
     baseline loads

#### The Most Defensible Evidence-Based Position

Based on ALL available evidence, the following hierarchy emerges:

1. **Ensure sufficient effort (proximity to failure)** - This is prerequisite.
   Sets not taken within ~1-3 RIR contribute minimally to hypertrophy.
   (Refalo 2023, Robinson 2024)

2. **Progress volume (sets) as the primary macro-variable** - The dose-response
   is well-established. Start near MEV, progress weekly, deload when
   approaching MRV. (Schoenfeld 2017, Pelland 2026, Israetel 2019)

3. **Use load/rep progression as the within-session micro-variable** - Double
   progression (reps up, then load up) within a rep range provides a
   session-to-session progression signal that ensures effort is maintained
   without requiring volume changes every session. (Plotkin 2022, Chaves 2024)

4. **Manage fatigue via deloads and volume cycling** - Volume cannot increase
   indefinitely. Periodic resets are necessary. (Amirthalingam 2017,
   Heaselgrave 2019)

This is essentially a hybrid model where volume is the primary mesocycle-level
progression variable and load/reps are the primary session-level progression
variables. This aligns with both the RP approach and practical double
progression, combining the strengths of each.

## 11. STIMULUS-TO-FATIGUE RATIO

The "stimulus to fatigue ratio" (SFR) concept, popularized by Israetel, is
not a formally studied variable in the literature but integrates several
well-established findings:

- **Each set produces a hypertrophic stimulus that diminishes with additional
  sets** (Krieger 2010, Amirthalingam 2017)
- **Each set also produces cumulative fatigue** that impairs recovery and
  subsequent training quality
- **Training closer to failure increases both stimulus AND fatigue** (Refalo
  2023, Robinson 2024)
- **The "optimal" zone is where stimulus is high relative to fatigue** - this
  is approximately 1-3 RIR for most sets, with ~4-6 hard sets per muscle per
  session

The SFR concept supports a hybrid model: use load/rep progression to maximize
per-set stimulus, and volume progression to accumulate total stimulus, while
monitoring fatigue to avoid exceeding MRV.
